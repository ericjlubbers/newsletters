THE DAILY DIGEST

Helen H. Richardson, The Denver Post

Good morning! Here are the top stories from The Denver Post for Monday, Sept. 21, 2015, including some you may have missed over the weekend.

1. Avalanche control gets an upgrade

The Colorado Department of Transportation has finally upgraded its ability to control avalanches along the state's mountainous highways thanks to the installation this month of 16 solar-powered Gazex exploders along Loveland and Berthoud passes. The technology finally moves us mostly beyond World War II-era technology of cannons and compressed-air launchers that shoot ordnance into avalanche zones. It's good news for business: Roads still must be shut down, but not for as long.

2. A war on police officers? That depends who you ask

Has public scrutiny of police actions around the United States encouraged detractors to act more aggressively toward officers? The concern has been heightened, locally, when someone shot at Aurora police hours after the department received a phone call threatening officers there and in Denver.

3. Fight between the feds and kombucha tea makers

Because fermentation is required to make kombucha, the drink generally includes a trace of alcohol. And because federal law has strict rules governing any drink with more than 0.5 percent alcohol by volume, one Boulder-based brewer is worried about running afoul of the feds, writes The Post's Mark K. Matthews.

4. A new entrance for the McNichols Building

The 1910 historic landmark — now a hub for social and cultural events — will close on Saturday as $6 million in renovations begin. This plan call for a new more graceful entrance to the structure (rather than the below-the-ground pit that currently exists) and a freight elevator, among other improvements.

5. Welcome to the marijuana election, where Colorado is the star

For the first time, pot is becoming a significant policy issue for Republican and Democratic candidates, partially because of shifting attitudes toward the drug and Colorado's status as a swing state.

6. MDMA to treat PTSD?

Yes. Boulder researchers hope that by 2021, MDMA — which stands for methylenedioxy-methamphetamine — can become a prescription medication so long as the therapy protocol is followed. So far, the study appears to be showing positive results.

7. Is Walt Weiss safe with the Rockies?

The Colorado Rockies manager told us previously that he wants to return to the team, which — over the weekend — managed to once again avoid another 100-loss season (hey, focus on the positive, right?). But new general manager Jeff Bridich isn't so sure about that. In a Sunday afternoon interview with MLB Network radio, he said he had not made a decision on Weiss' future. Weiss was hired by former GM Dan O'Dowd, whom Bridich replaced during the off-season.

8. What is the Broncos' offense?

Speaking of uncertainty in Denver sports — this Broncos offense. What is it? If anything, Thursday's late win over the Kansas City Chiefs exposed its flaws — and what happens when you just let quarterback Peyton Manning be Peyton Manning. As columnist Woody Paige notes: "You don't make Monet paint dogs playing poker, Mozart compose radio jingles, Michelangelo sculpt polyresin figurines, Maazel conduct a kazoo band, Mick sing ABBA, Monk play 'Chopsticks.' And you don't make Manning huddle up, get under center and hand off the football."

9. A revitalizing of West Colfax Avenue

With the redevelopment of the old St. Anthony Hospital campus picking up speed, retail interest — including Alamo Drafthouse and Little Man Ice Cream — is increasing along West Colfax Avenue. Commercial real estate reporter Emilie Rusch looks at what this means for a thoroughfare best known for its jumble of used-car dealerships, repair shops and motels.

10. At the Emmys, history is made — despite Andy Samberg

Host Andy Samberg fell flat, but history was made anyway, with Viola Davis becoming the first black woman to win best actress in a drama series for her role in "How to Get Away with Murder." "So many shows, so little time," Samberg sang. "So many jokes, so few real laughs," retorts Denver Post TV critic Joanne Ostrow. Samberg's "Mad Men" parody fell flat, and his childish humor dragged down the 67th annual show, Ostrow writes.

WHAT ELSE WE'RE READING

— A battalion ravaged by suicide

The New York Times has a devastating report on a Marine battalion whose members served in a violent region in Afghanistan and are now seemingly being stalked by suicide. At least 13 members have killed themselves, and those left behind — struggling with demons and unable to get help from VA — are left wondering: Am I next?

— Why the best war reporter of a generation had to suddenly stop

C.J. Chivers spent more than a decade covering the worst wars of our time — Libya, Afghanistan, Iraq — for The New York Times. Esquire looks at what finally made the former Marine finally step away from the battlefield.

— A death at Torrey Pines

The Atlantic explores a long-cold San Diego murder case that's been solved with DNA evidence. But questions of contamination, including a result that positively identified a worker in the lab that tested the samples, have authorities questioning the resolution.
