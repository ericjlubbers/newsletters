THE DAILY DIGEST

Good morning! Today we look at an innovative homeless program cutting staff, what some cities are doing about Columbus Day and, of course, how the Denver Broncos have managed to get away with five wins this season. Let's go!

"Somehow, we've won five games, the offense has contributed some to that."

The Big Stuff

It's Columbus Day (to some). Today is Columbus Day, a bank holiday in the U.S., but an increasing number of cities, like Albuquerque, Portland and St. Paul, are reviving a movement to change the holiday to Indigenous Peoples Day.

An innovative homeless facility just took a big cut. The Fort Lyon Supportive Residential community houses more than 200 formely homeless men and women in a unique extended-stay program that includes counseling, substance abuse treatment and job training. But a "miscommunication" about the amount of money available for the program left administrators scrambling to cut $400,000, which ultimately came at the cost of 7 of the 17 full-time case managers. "These are people we've grown to trust and confide in," said Peggy Baugher, 61, a resident whose case manager was among those laid off. "I've been a heroin addict for 45 years and I quit using for 60 days here, which I've never done in my life." Read John Wenzel's full report here.

Kidney care is good business. DaVita, the Denver-based, kidney-focused health care giant, is expanding its headquarters into a brand new skyscraper in LoDo. Beyond a shiny new building, the company will be bringing 800 new jobs averaging about $89,000/year.

Speaking of health care, a Denver state senator is trying to make Colorado a single-payer state. Sen. Irene Aguilar, a medical doctor, is currently gathering signatures to get Initiative 20 on the ballot next year. The Initiative would unwind the current state system, which basically acts as a marketplace for private insurance, and replace it with "ColoradoCare," a state-run insurance plan that would directly pay medical costs the way private insurers and Medicare do now. Supporters say it would cost less per person and remove deductibles and co-pays on most basic care, while detractors say it's an unproven system based on a large tax increase.
