Discovery of young man's body ends one mystery, leaves many questions

"We'll never really know what happened to him. It's a horror story in my mind to imagine what my brother must have gone through."

Kate Maddux

After years of searching for 18-year-old Josh Maddux, who had gone missing in 2008, his family was horrified to learn that a body found wedged inside a stone chimney of a cabin just a quarter-mile from their home was their son and brother. The El Paso County coroner, who ruled the death accidental, described the death as "very confusing" as there were no signs of trauma, yet the body was in the fetal position and clad only in a thermal shirt with his feet facing down. He believes Josh climbed into the chimney and became stuck, but the owner of the vacant cabin doesn't buy that story. Read Jesse Paul's full report here.

The Big Stuff

Saying goodbye to a Mexican institution. Rosa Linda's Mexican Cafe will serve its last ladle of green chile on Oct. 30 after three decades of bringing Mexican family recipes to Denver's Highland neighborhood. Founder Rosa Linda Aguirre says what will "hurt the most" about closing is having to cancel the restaurant's annual Thanksgiving Feast to the Needy, which we filmed in 2011.

If you name a horse Houdini, you'd beter expect him to escape. This is a fantastic opening line for story, for so many reasons: "A search for a popular horse that escaped from a Douglas County ranch has ended with the help of a drone." First off, what qualifies a horse as popular? Secondly, here's a situation where the drone technology proves its worth. Groups of volunteers had failed to find the horse by foot and vehicle, but a drone company's custom flyer outfitted with a $3,000 infrared camera found Houdini (suffering from minor barbed wire wounds) quickly.

By The Numbers

1 in 11

That's the ratio of industrial buildings in Denver that are full of marijuana, according to a study by the real estate firm CBRE. That rounds out to at least 3.7 million square feet of industrial space, especially around the Mousetrap (the I-25/I-70 junction for you recent Colorado imports). The firm credits marijuana cultivation for kickstarting the local commercial real estate market, which is great for landlords, but not so great for nonprofits like Toys for Tots.
